Absolute Positioning

Elements below use absolute positioning, which takes them out of the normal document flow and places them relative to the nearest ancestor with a non-static position.

This paragraph sits in the normal flow. The absolutely positioned elements that follow will not affect its layout.

This paragraph also flows normally—notice how it ignores the space taken by the red boxes and the image.

When to Use absolute

  • For tooltips, pop-ups, or dropdown menus that must overlay other content.
  • When precise placement is needed, e.g., positioning icons relative to a container.

When Not to Use absolute

  • For large layout sections that should adapt responsively—absolute elements don't push or pull siblings.
  • When you need elements to participate in the document flow, e.g., vertically stacking content.

Precautions & Best Practices

  • Always give the container a position: relative; so your absolute children are scoped correctly.
  • Use z-index thoughtfully to manage stacking order.
  • Be mindful of responsiveness—fixed pixel offsets can break on smaller screens.
